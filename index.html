PocketVR USB-C Module

Binocular · central screen · beam-splitter prism · slides open · phone held upside down

Fig. 1 — Closed State · Front View · Attached to Phone Bottom

Closed state. Module is 60mm wide and 25mm tall — slightly narrower than the phone. Both lens barrels are retracted flush with the housing. The central VR screen sits hidden inside. A single button on top triggers the spring mechanism. The phone is held upside down — USB-C port connects at the top.

Fig. 2 — Open State · Springs to Both Sides · Press to Face

Open state. One button press — both arms spring out simultaneously via a spring-loaded slide mechanism. Each arm carries a Fresnel lens barrel. The central module contains the VR screen and a 45° beam-splitter prism that divides the screen image simultaneously to both lenses. IPD (eye spacing) is naturally set by the slide stop position — can be made adjustable 58–74mm. Total open width matches the phone width (~130mm).

Fig. 3 — Cross-Section Top View · Optical Path Through Beam Splitter

Optical cross-section (side view). The beam splitter prism is a glass cube with a half-silvered 45° mirror inside. Screen light from below hits the diagonal — left half transmits straight through upward to the left Fresnel lens, right half reflects 90° downward to the right Fresnel lens. Both eyes receive their image from the single central screen simultaneously. Each barrel extends outward (sideways in the top view) when the spring releases.

Fig. 4 — Usage · Phone Upside Down · Press to Face · Optional Strap

Two usage modes. Mode 1: phone held upside down with both hands, module pressed to face — quick, hands-on, no setup. Mode 2: click-in elastic strap attaches to phone side rails, goes around head — hands-free for longer sessions. Strap removes in one second by unclipping from phone sides.

Key design decisions:

Why upside down? Phone cameras go to the bottom (out of the way). USB-C connects at the top to the module — natural position. Hands grip the phone body below the module — stable, natural hold.

Why one central screen + prism? One high-quality screen is cheaper and better than two small screens. The beam splitter cube (same technology as in professional cameras and microscopes) splits the light perfectly with no quality loss. Screen sits 20–25mm from both lenses — optimal focal distance.

Why spring-loaded sliding arms? One button, instant open. No manual adjustment needed for most users (IPD 58–74mm covered by slide stop). Closes flat for pocket carry. Same mechanism as a retractable badge holder or camera flash — proven, cheap to manufacture.

Fig. 5 — Silicone Face Seal · Full Perimeter · Open State · Front + Side View

Silicone face seal — the critical upgrade. When the module springs open, the continuous silicone seal covers the entire perimeter — top (forehead), both sides, bottom (cheeks). Two lens holes allow the barrels to protrude through. A shaped nose bridge cutout fits naturally without pressure. When pressed to the face the 12mm seal compresses to ~6mm, blocking 100% of ambient light — same as a professional VR headset. When closed, the silicone is held flat by small magnets — no bulk in pocket. Material: medical-grade Shore A30 silicone — soft, hypoallergenic, washable and replaceable.

Why this is better than individual eyecups:

Individual rubber eyecups (as on binoculars) require you to position both eyes perfectly against two separate seals. A single continuous face seal is much more comfortable — it conforms to your entire face shape in one contact surface, no gap between the eyes, no pressure points. The nose bridge cutout means no squeezing on the nose. This is exactly how Meta Quest, PlayStation VR, and Apple Vision Pro seal the face — one continuous foam/silicone contact surface.

Manufacturing: Single injection-moulded silicone piece. One mould, one part. Attaches to the module frame via a magnetic snap ring — user can remove and wash it. Replacement seals available as accessories.
