PocketVR — Claim 2

Asymmetric VR glasses · two mirrors inside phone · left mirror routes image to back glass · glasses lens completes left eye path

Fig. 1 — Cross-Section · Both Mirror Systems Inside Phone · Side View Cut Through

Both mirror systems shown side by side. The phone contains two 45° mirrors — one on each side. Both work identically: screen light travels upward, hits the mirror, bends 90° toward the back glass, exits through an aperture. Right side (Claim 1): the barrel eyepiece is built into the phone — already there. Left side (Claim 2): the aperture is there, the mirror is there, but the lens is NOT built in — the glasses frame provides it. The connector arm holds the glasses lens precisely over the left aperture. Zero electronics in glasses — purely passive optics.

Fig. 2 — Phone Back · Landscape · Two Apertures · Glasses Dock Left · Barrel Right

Phone back viewed from outside. Left side (near USB-C): the left-eye aperture — a circular hole in the back glass where the mirror's redirected light exits. Three magnetic dock points hold the glasses frame precisely aligned over this aperture. Right side (near cameras): the built-in barrel eyepiece from Claim 1 — its own mirror routes right-eye screen light through the same mechanism. The glasses connector arm spans the full width between left aperture and right eyepiece dock.

Fig. 3 — Asymmetric Glasses Frame · Lens Aligns Over Left Aperture · Zero Electronics

The asymmetric glasses frame. Left side: Fresnel lens housing that aligns precisely over the left-eye aperture in the back glass. The magnetic snap positions the lens within ±0.5mm — critical for optical alignment. Right side: long connector arm spanning the full phone width, ending in a magnetic dock at the camera cluster / eyepiece end. The frame contains zero electronics — no battery, no screen, no processor. Manufacturing cost: ~$9–12.

Fig. 4 — Four Stages · Phone Landscape · Mirror Routes Left-Eye Image to Back Glass

Four stages to full stereo VR. Stage 1: Phone works normally, both mirrors inside ready. Stage 2: Barrel pulled, Hall sensor fires, screen splits, right eye active via right mirror + barrel. Left mirror now routing left-eye image to the back-glass aperture — aperture glowing, waiting for glasses lens. Stage 3: Glasses brought to phone back, lens self-aligns over aperture via magnets. Stage 4: Both eyes active. Full stereo VR. Zero extra battery.

Claim 2 — What makes this patentable and novel:

The dual-mirror architecture: The phone contains TWO identical 45° mirror systems. Right mirror (Claim 1) routes the right-eye image to the built-in barrel eyepiece. Left mirror (Claim 2) routes the left-eye image to a second aperture in the back glass. The glasses frame completes the left-eye optical path by providing a Fresnel lens that docks over this aperture. This specific dual-mirror phone architecture with one path completed externally by passive glasses is not found in any patent.

Zero electronics in glasses: The glasses frame contains no battery, no screen, no processor, no wireless. It is a purely passive optical and mechanical assembly. Patent addition as dependent claim (f): "wherein the glasses frame contains no battery, processor, screen, or active electronic components — all power, processing, sensing, and display functions being provided entirely by the smartphone device."

Landscape geometry + wide baseline: Phone held horizontally. Left aperture near USB-C end. Right eyepiece near camera cluster. Wide baseline ~130mm — software places each eye's image at the exact screen position to match its lens. Wide baseline creates enhanced depth perception. Patent addition as dependent claim (g).
